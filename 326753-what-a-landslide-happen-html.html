Landslide meaning

A landslide also referred to as landslip, is a geological process that involves many types of downhill earth movements. In essence, landslides are masses of rock, earth, or debris that are moving slowly or quickly down the slope. These occurrences are unique from mountainous debris flow and catastrophic rock avalanche.

Scientists refer to landslides as a mass wasting process in which gravitational force influences the downhill movement of rocks and soil. On the other hand, the rapid movement of debris is called avalanche.

What is the difference between a landslide and a mudslide?

Unlike commonly slow-moving landslides, mudslides move at relatively higher speeds. It is correct to say that mudslides are fast-moving landslides that often flow in a channel.

Human causes

Some human activities may be deadly because they activate landslides. These include:

  • Excavating the slope or its toe
  • Deforestation/ clearance of vegetation cover
  • Mining/quarrying
  • Water leakage from utilities
  • Undercutting
  • Constructions, blasting, building, and engineering works.

Geological causes

At times, natural occurrences without human intervention can lead to landslides. These includes:

  • Sensitive, weak, or weathered materials
  • Discontinuity that is adversely oriented, for instance fault, schistosity, and bedding among others

Morphological causes

They include:

  • Tectonic plate movements
  • Subterranean erosion
  • Weathering freeze and thaw
  • Vegetation removal either by fire or drought
  • Glacial rebound

Scientists maintain that most cases of Kenyan landslides are triggered by both human activities as well as fluctuations in water levels. For this reason, slopes saturated with water often stand a higher risk of initiating a severe landslide.

Areas in Kenya that are prone to landslides

The Rift Valley region is the most affected in Kenya because of frequent earth tremors that destabilize debris masses on the steep edges. Upon shaking, the ground opens up absorbing excess water, which results in saturation.

The steep slopes of the Rift valley extend up to 80 meters, with the region experiencing rains of up to 1, 200 mm every year. Profiles show the areas have volcanic soils that become saturated with water when it rains heavily. The higher population in the region also plays a significant role in the ongoing landslides.

How do I prevent landslides?

There are various ways to prevent the occurrence of landslides

  • Proper land use and care play a more significant role in preventing landslides. There is, therefore, a need for farmers within affected areas to focus on crops that mitigate slides. Such may include trees as well as plants.
  • People residing in landslides prone areas should avoid establishing their residence on risky slopes.
  • Directing water away, lowering surface irrigation, channeling off the groundwater to prevent over-saturation.
  • Planting deep-rooted trees along the slopes
