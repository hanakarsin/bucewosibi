Kenyans Express Frustrations after Safaricom Temporarily Disrupts Mpesa Services

Posted by Almeda Bohannan on Monday, January 1, 0001
  • Many Kenyans trooped to their social media platforms to complain over the disruption of Mpesa services
  • Some said they had been held hostage in various places as they could not pay for their services
  • Safaricom, however, assured its clients that efforts to restore the services were underway

Kenyans have taken to their social media platforms to express frustrations and inconveniences after telecommunication giant Safaricom's Mpesa services stopped working.

Citing how their day-to-day activities had been affected by the Mpesa disruption, many called on Safaricom to expedite the fixing of the hitch which is costing many time and money.

In the complaints on social media platforms, some shared how they were shopping but could not pay for the goods or services owing to the disruption of Mpesa services.

One Kenyan said he was stranded in a hotel because he could not pay the bill.

"Safaricom, some of us are stranded in hotels," he said.

Collins Arap Bett said:

"Never been held hostage like this before. The lady at the station trusted me and asked me to pay once the service is up and send her the message. Still waiting for mpesa to be resuscitated."

Qaaliabdirahman said:

"I sent an Mpesa transaction for food thrice at Freys. Mpesa delays should give notice prior. What kind of embarrassment is this?"

On his part, Wanjihia lamented he had been at the petrol station for 15 minutes because of the delay.

Ominde Okoth Rainbow said:

"Mpesa is so critical to this economy that it should never be allowed to go down."

In its response, the telco asked its clients to be patient as the Mpesa outage gets fixed.

"Our sincere apologies for the inconvenience. We are currently experiencing a Mpesa outage and we are working to restore services as soon as possible," the telecommunication giant said.

Restructuring of Safaricom

In a separate report, TUKO.co.ke reported the Central Organisation of Trade Unions (COTU) expressed its reservations over Safaricom's plan to restructure its management mode.

In the plan, the telecommunications giant sought to break the hierarchical structure whilst doing away with some of its employees, a move that would leave its over 6000 workers troubled.

The Francis Atwoli led union expressed concerns about the prospects of job security of the workers, especially after they were required to re-apply for their positions.
