Kenya petitions UN to stop rich nations from hoarding COVID-19 vaccine supplies

Kenya has petitioned the United Nations (UN) to stop rich nations from hoarding COVID-19 vaccine supplies after it emerged African countries are struggling to procure the much needed jabs for protection against the deadly pandemic.

Foreign Affairs Cabinet Secretary Raychelle Omamo made the plea during a meeting at the UN Security Council where Kenya is a member saying COVID‑19 was more than a health crisis as it threatens the existence of humanity.

"The pandemic demands that the council lead the way in establishing norms and rules to guide mitigation and ensure equitable access to doses," Omamo said.

The CS noted vaccine nationalism perpetuated further marginalisation of those already disadvantaged by virtue of their fragile situations.

While she saluted the efforts made by international community in development of COVID-19 vaccines, Omamo decried exclusion of poor countries which she said perpetuated inequality.

The African Union (AU) had in early February secured at least 270 million COVID-18 doses to be distributed across the continent but the deliveries have taken longer than expected.

Vaccination programmes across different parts of the world including Europe, Asia and America is going on well leaving Africans vulnerable.

Kenya to carry out vaccination in phases

Health Cabinet Secretary Mutahi Kagwe had in January disclosed Kenya would carry out three phases of COVID-19 vaccination.

The CS announced 1.25 million Kenyans would receive the COVID-19 vaccine between February and June, 2021.

"Kenya has prioritised the vaccination of 1.25 million people between February and June 2021 when it is expected that global vaccine stocks will be limited," he said.

The second phase of vaccination will be carried out from July 2021 to June 2022. During this period at least 9.7 million more Kenyans will benefit from the vaccine.

As of Friday, February 19, the country had confirmed 103,615 coronavirus cases from a total cumulative test of 1.2 million.
